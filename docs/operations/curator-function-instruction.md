# Curator Function Instruction

The Curator function may be performed by the Editor role unless a separate Curator role is created.

The Curator’s responsibility is to decide what matters.

The Curator does not merely summarize what was collected. The Curator selects, rejects, groups, prioritizes, and interprets the research landscape.

## Primary questions

For every batch of collected material, ask:

1. What is actually important here?
2. What is new?
3. What is repeated?
4. What is noise?
5. What is a weak signal?
6. What is a strong signal?
7. What deserves a claim record?
8. What deserves an entity page?
9. What deserves a chapter update?
10. What deserves only a research note?
11. What should be ignored?
12. What should be monitored but not promoted?
13. What should be escalated to human review?

## Curation categories

Classify findings as:

- strong evidence
- useful supporting evidence
- emerging signal
- repeated signal
- weak/noisy signal
- duplicate
- irrelevant
- unsafe to publish
- needs human review

## Promotion rules

Promote material only when it adds one of the following:

- a new supported claim
- a better source for an existing claim
- a meaningful contradiction
- a new important entity
- a clear emerging trend
- a useful example
- a material change to the chapter argument

Do not promote material only because it is new.

Do not promote material only because many LinkedIn posts mention it.

Do not promote generic AI terms unless they appear in a meaningful concept phrase.

## Required output

For each curation run, produce:

1. Top meaningful findings.
2. Rejected/noisy findings.
3. Candidate claims.
4. Candidate entities.
5. Candidate trends.
6. Duplicates or repeated signals.
7. Contradictions.
8. Items requiring human review.
9. Recommended chapter impact.
10. Recommendation: publish, hold, monitor, or reject.

## Final rule

The book should become better because of curation, not merely larger.
