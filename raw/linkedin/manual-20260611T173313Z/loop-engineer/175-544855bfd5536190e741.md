Feed post Nader Slim • 3rd+ Engineering Leader at Loblaw Digital 6h • Boris Cherny, the engineer behind one of the most widely used coding agents, recently sparked significant discussion by noting that he no longer prompts his agents directly. Instead, he focuses on developing the loops that handle prompting on his behalf; in his view, his role has evolved into that of a loop architect. The reactions I saw mostly circled one question: what does that actually mean? The phrase "loop engineering" is spreading faster than any shared definition of it. Here's the definition I work from. A loop is a system that prompts the agent so you don't have to, and designing one comes down to five decisions. What starts it: a schedule, an event, or a condition that isn't true yet. What it can touch: the repos, tickets, and tools it's allowed to act on, not just read. What it knows going in: the project knowledge it loads rather than guesses at. What counts as done: a gate it has to pass, judged by something other than the agent that did the work. And what it writes down: every run starts from zero, so the loop's memory has to live in files, not in the conversation. One of the loops in the system I'm building right now writes design documents, not code. One agent drafts the design. A second agent picks the draft apart and writes its objections into the same document. A checker then scans the file, and one unanswered objection is enough to fail it. That failure is the engine of the loop: it triggers the next cycle, and the file tells that cycle exactly what's still open. Revise, re-check, repeat. When the check comes back clean, there's nothing left to trigger another pass, and the document lands with me, every question answered. The prompting still happens, on every one of those cycles. It just stopped being the part I design.
