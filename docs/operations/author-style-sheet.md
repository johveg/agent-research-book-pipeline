# Author Style Sheet

This style sheet applies to all book chapters and public-facing explanatory pages.

## Audience

The audience is technically literate.

Assume readers may be:

- operators
- architects
- engineers
- platform builders
- AI practitioners
- technical executives
- researchers
- system designers

Do not assume they are beginners.

Do not assume they know the internal project context.

## Voice

The voice should be:

- clear
- grounded
- analytical
- practical
- serious
- readable
- specific
- source-aware
- lightly opinionated when justified

The voice should not be:

- promotional
- vague
- generic
- breathless
- motivational
- academic for its own sake
- LinkedIn-style
- AI-generated filler

## Preferred sentence style

Use direct sentences.

Prefer concrete nouns and verbs.

Avoid long abstract passages.

Use examples where helpful.

Use short sections with clear headings.

## Argument style

Good sections should usually:

1. Name the problem.
2. Define the concept.
3. Explain why it matters.
4. Show what the evidence supports.
5. Explain the practical implication.
6. State limits or uncertainty.

## Evidence style

Distinguish:

- observation
- evidence
- interpretation
- opinion
- open question

Use careful language.

Good phrases:

- “The available evidence suggests..."
- “This appears to be..."
- “A safer interpretation is..."
- “A weak signal is..."
- “The current sources do not yet support..."
- “This should not be overstated."

Avoid overconfident phrases unless fully supported:

- “This proves..."
- “Everyone is..."
- “The industry has moved..."
- “It is obvious that..."
- “There is no doubt..."

## Forbidden or discouraged words

Avoid unless directly quoted:

- revolutionary
- game-changing
- unlock
- unleash
- transformative
- disruptive
- paradigm shift
- next-generation
- groundbreaking
- magical
- seamless
- frictionless
- cutting-edge
- future-proof

## Treatment of social media

Social media can show:

- language
- sentiment
- early signals
- practitioner attention
- emerging examples

Social media should not by itself prove:

- adoption
- consensus
- correctness
- maturity
- market size
- technical reliability

## Treatment of internal project examples

Internal project examples may be used as implementation examples.

They must not be generalized into market-wide claims.

Use wording like:

- “In this project, the pattern appears as..."
- “A local implementation example is..."
- “This illustrates the kind of loop being discussed..."

Do not use wording like:

- “This proves the industry needs..."
- “This confirms everyone will..."
- “This is the new standard..."

## Chapter ending style

Prefer endings that clarify implications.

Good endings:

- “The practical implication is..."
- “The unresolved question is..."
- “The next design problem is..."
- “The evidence is not yet strong enough to conclude..."

Avoid motivational endings.

## Final style rule

The book should sound like it was written by a careful technical author, not generated by a marketing engine.
