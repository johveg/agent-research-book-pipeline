## Visible text

For about two years, getting something out of a coding agent meant holding it the whole time. You typed a prompt, read what came back, typed the next one. You were the loop. Addy Osmani has a piece this week on what's replacing that, and the line that stuck is from Boris Cherny at Anthropic: "I don't prompt Claude anymore. I have loops running that prompt Claude." Instead of nudging the agent turn by turn, you build a small system that finds the work, hands it out, checks it, writes down what's done, and decides the next thing. Then you let that system poke the agents instead of you. What surprised me is how quietly this stopped being a tooling story. A year ago a loop was a pile of bash you maintained forever. Now the pieces ship inside the products, and they're roughly the same pieces whether you're in Codex or Claude Code. But here's the part worth sitting with. The loop doesn't change who you are at the work. Two people can build the identical loop and get opposite results. One uses it to move faster on something they understand deeply. The other uses it to avoid understanding it at all. The loop can't tell the difference. I wrote a couple weeks back about the orchestration tax, how your review bandwidth, not the tool, is the real ceiling on how many agents you can run. Loop engineering doesn't lift that ceiling. It just makes it easier to forget the ceiling is there. The leverage point moved. The judgment didn't. So build the loops. Just build them like someone who plans to stay the engineer, not the person who presses go.
